Roles and responsibilities

Learning Specialist role

The role and responsibilities of your learning specialist:

  • Evaluate your disability documentation to carefully consider the functional limitations
  • Assess your needs which may impact the academic or campus experience.
  • Consider the program of study, and course requirements.
  • Establish a reasonable and individualized academic accommodations plan which may include: Exam accommodations, Teaching accommodations, Transcriptions accommodations and Learning strategies.
  • Offer learning strategies to build and improve your academic skills based on challenges and strengths.
  • Assist you, your professors and other support staff to ensure that your approved accommodations are implemented, while maintaining your confidentiality.
  • Providing you with the pertinent information about services and supports available to students with disabilities and the many other campus resources.
Professor role

The role and responsibilities of your professor:

  • Working collaboratively with Academic Accommodations and you to implement the approved accommodation plan. This may include providing exam information (date, time and duration), working with our office to deliver adapted exams, and offering teaching accommodations.
  • Making sure that the approved accommodations respect academic integrity, and maintain essential program requirements and skills.
Student role

Your student role and responsibilities:

  • Adhering to the academic standards established by the University of Ottawa.
  • Complying with all Academic Accommodation's procedures and University of Ottawa policies
  • Contacting Academic Accommodations to discuss academic needs and supports in a timely manner as well as providing relative documentation from a medical professional.
  • Follow up on learning strategies to build and improve your academic skills based on challenges and strengths

What difference should you expect if coming from High School?

Your student role has many differences between high school and university, such as:

  • You are responsible for your own learning
  • No one will remind you to attend classes or do your schoolwork.
  • You may find yourself spending more time studying than attending lectures.
  • Professors (and possibly teaching assistants) have weekly office hours. This can be helpful if you have questions and need further clarification. However you will not see them as often as you likely saw your high school teachers.
  • Many campus services will be available for you if you require assistance with learning tips for success and staying motivated. Stay tuned for emails from your faculty as well.

  • Your schedule will look quite different
  • Depending on your program, you may have 15 hours of class a week and get to pick your course schedule.
  • Classes can vary between 1.5 and 3 hours.
  • There will be gaps between classes.
  • You may have to travel to a different building to get to your next class.

  • You will know the course schedules and generally what to expect at the start of the course
  • On the first day of class, you will get a course schedule and know all your assignments, readings and test dates for most of the term. Your faculty will announce final exam dates later.
  • A term runs for about 3 1/2 months, so it is common to have 3 or 4 assessments in a term. Assignments or quizzes may also appear.
